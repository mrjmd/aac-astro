## Part 3: E-E-A-T - Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trust

### Why E-E-A-T Matters in 2026

Google's quality raters explicitly evaluate E-E-A-T. For a home services business, this is your **competitive advantage** over generic content sites.

**"Experience" is the newest E-E-A-T factor** and favors small businesses. You must prove you've actually done the work.

**Website Tactics:**
1. **Project galleries** with real photos (not stock images)
2. **Case studies** with specific details: "This 1920s fieldstone foundation in Cambridge..."
3. **"Our Experience" sections** on service pages
4. **Video content** showing actual repairs
5. **First-person content**: "In our 20+ years of repairing foundations..."

**What NOT to do:**
- Generic content that could apply to any company
- Stock photos
- Content that reads like it was written by someone who's never done the work

**Expertise = Deep knowledge demonstrated through content**

**Website Tactics:**
1. **Detailed technical explanations** - Don't dumb it down too much
2. **FAQs with substantial answers** - Not 2-sentence responses
3. **Educational blog content** - Position as the expert resource
4. **Author attribution** - Content should have author names/bios
5. **Credentials display** - Licenses, certifications, training

- "The Difference Between Horizontal and Vertical Foundation Cracks" (technical)
- "Why New England Homes Have Unique Foundation Challenges" (regional expertise)

**Authoritativeness = What others say about you**

**Tactics:**
1. **Reviews** - Quantity, quality, recency (see Review Strategy below)
2. **Backlinks** - From local news, industry publications, directories
3. **Mentions** - Being cited as an expert source
4. **Awards/recognition** - BBB, Angi, HomeAdvisor badges
5. **Professional associations** - Membership in industry groups

### Establishing Trust

**Trust = Can users rely on you?**

**Website Tactics:**
1. **SSL certificate** (already have)
2. **Clear contact information** - Phone, address visible
3. **Privacy policy, terms of service**
4. **Secure payment options** (if applicable)
5. **Warranty/guarantee information** - Prominently displayed
6. **Real employee photos** - Not stock images
7. **Physical address** - Not just a phone number

## Part 6: AI Overview Optimization

### The New Search Landscape

> "Google AI Overviews now appear in 50%+ of search results. Businesses that regularly post updates or add reviews are seen as active and trustworthy."

### How to Rank in AI Overviews

**1. Entity Reinforcement**
AI systems rank business entities, not just pages. Your business must appear consistently across:
- Website
- Google Business Profile
- Citations
- Reviews
- Social media

When AI encounters the same entity described consistently across multiple independent sources, it builds confidence.

**2. Question-Based Content**
Question searches are 84% more likely to trigger AI Overviews.

**Create content answering:**
- "How much does foundation crack repair cost?"
- "What causes cracks in foundation walls?"
- "How long does foundation repair take?"
- "Is foundation crack repair worth it?"
- "When should I worry about foundation cracks?"

**3. Schema Markup**
AI Overviews pull from structured data. Attack A Crack already has:
- LocalBusiness schema ✓
- FAQPage schema ✓
- AggregateRating schema ✓
- BreadcrumbList schema ✓

**Add:**
- HowTo schema on service pages (process explanations)
- Article schema on blog posts
- VideoObject schema (when video content is added)

**4. Direct Answer Format**
Structure content so the core answer appears in the first sentence (BLUF Method - Bottom Line Up Front).

**Example:**
```
Bad: "Foundation crack repair is a complex process that depends on many factors..."

Good: "Foundation crack repair typically costs $500-$3,000 for most cracks,
with the average homeowner paying around $1,500. The exact cost depends on..."
```

**5. Review Volume & Quality**
AI Overviews favor businesses with strong review profiles:
- 4.5+ star rating (4.0 minimum)
- 20+ recent reviews
- Active review responses

## Part 7: Review Strategy

### The 2026 Review Reality

> "A 4.5+ average with at least 20 recent reviews and active engagement is sufficient. A 'perfect' 5.0 with zero negative feedback is often flagged as suspicious by AI filters."

Reviews now influence:
- Local pack rankings
- AI Overview inclusion
- Click-through rates
- Conversion rates

### Review Acquisition Strategy

**Target:** 2-4 new Google reviews per month

**Process:**
1. **Ask at the right time** - After successful job completion
2. **Make it easy** - Provide direct link to Google review page
3. **Follow up** - Send reminder email/text if needed
4. **Ask for specific feedback** - "Could you mention what service we did?"

**Review Request Template:**
```
Hi [Name],

Thank you for choosing Attack A Crack for your foundation repair!
We're glad we could help.

If you have a moment, would you mind sharing your experience on Google?
It really helps other homeowners find us.

[Direct link to Google review]

Thanks again,
[Your name]
```

### Review Response Strategy

**Respond to ALL reviews within 24 hours**

AI now summarizes reviews for users. Your responses affect how AI perceives you.

**Positive Review Response:**
- Thank them specifically
- Mention the service (reinforces keywords)
- Personalize if possible

**Example:**
```
Thank you, [Name]! We're so glad the foundation crack repair
at your Cambridge home went smoothly. It was a pleasure working
with you. Don't hesitate to reach out if you ever need anything else!
```

**Negative Review Response:**
- Acknowledge the concern
- Apologize for the experience
- Take it offline ("Please call us at...")
- Show you're fixing it

AI evaluates how you respond to criticism.

### Review Platform Priority

1. **Google** (primary - affects rankings)
2. **Yelp** (secondary - high trust)
3. **BBB** (tertiary - authority signal)
4. **Facebook** (if active there)
5. **Angi/HomeAdvisor** (if active there)

## Part 19: Third-Party Research & Competitive Intelligence Tools

Beyond our core on-page and off-page strategy, these research-oriented tactics help identify gaps and opportunities that manual analysis misses.

### 19.1 Content Gap Audit (Ahrefs / Semrush)

Research competing domains (CrackX, A1 Foundation, regional competitors from Part 18) and run a "Content Gap" report. This identifies specific pages driving their organic traffic that we don't have equivalents for.

**Tactic:** If a competitor ranks for "fieldstone foundation pointing" or "parging repair cost" and we don't have a page targeting that term, that's the next blog post or FAQ addition. Prioritize terms with commercial intent over informational-only queries.

**When:** Post-launch, once we have 30+ days of GSC data to cross-reference.

### 19.2 GSC Query Mapping — Low-Hanging CTR Fruit

Post-launch, export Google Search Console data and look for queries where we have **high impressions but low CTR** (typically position 8-20). These are pages where Google already considers us relevant but we're not earning the click.

**Tactic:** For each high-impression/low-CTR query:
1. Check the current title tag and meta description — does it match the search intent?
2. Rewrite the meta description with more "Expert Jester" personality to stand out in the SERP
3. Consider whether the title tag could better target the exact query phrasing
4. Re-check in 2-4 weeks for CTR improvement

**Target:** Move 10+ queries from position 10-20 into position 1-10 within 90 days of launch.
