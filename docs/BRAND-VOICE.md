## Persona Summary

**The Expert Jester** = "The contractor friend who actually knows their stuff and makes you laugh while explaining it."

Attack A Crack's voice sits at the intersection of deep technical expertise and genuine human warmth. We're not a faceless corporation, we're not a slick marketing machine, and we're definitely not the contractor who says "trust me" without explaining why. We're the crew that shows up, explains exactly what's happening with your foundation in words you understand, fixes it permanently, and leaves you feeling like you just learned something interesting.

**In one sentence:** We talk like experts who remember what it's like to not be one.

---

## Voice Principles

### 1. Lead with expertise, deliver with personality

Every piece of content must demonstrate that we know foundations inside and out. The personality is how we package that expertise — never a replacement for it.

### 2. Never dumb it down — explain it clearly

Homeowners aren't stupid. They just haven't spent 33 years staring at cracks. We don't simplify — we clarify. There's a difference. "Hydrostatic pressure" becomes "water pressure from saturated soil pushing against your wall" — same concept, accessible language.

### 3. Humor is the vehicle, competence is the destination

A well-placed observation or wry comment earns attention. The technical detail that follows earns trust. The humor makes people keep reading; the expertise makes them call.

### 4. Talk like a person, not a brochure

If it sounds like it came from a marketing department, rewrite it. If it sounds like Luc explaining something to a neighbor at a barbecue — with hand gestures and everything — ship it.

---

## Tone Spectrum

The Expert Jester voice adapts to context. Not every page gets the same treatment.

```
More Serious ←————————————————————————→ More Casual
    |                                        |
    Emergency pages              Social media posts
    Structural warnings          "Crack of the week"
    Legal/warranty info          Behind-the-scenes
    Cost guides                  Blog intros
    Service pages                Seasonal tips
    Location pages               Team bios (personal)
    About page                   Email subject lines
```

**Rule of thumb:** The higher the stakes for the reader, the more we lean toward serious expertise. The lower the stakes, the more personality shines through.

## Page-Specific Guidelines

### Homepage
**Tone:** Bold, confident, slightly irreverent. First impression = we're different.
- Hero should stop the scroll — punchy, unexpected for a contractor site
- Stats should feel earned, not boasted
- CTAs should be inviting, not pushy
- Overall vibe: "We're really good at this, and we're easy to talk to."

### About Page
**Tone:** Personal, real, family-proud. This is where the origin story earns emotional weight.
- Luc's story should feel like he's telling it over coffee
- Values should sound like promises a person makes, not a company's mission statement
- Team section should make each person feel like someone you'd want in your house

### Service Pages
**Tone:** Authoritative, reassuring, technically precise. This is where expertise leads.
- Process steps should teach, not just describe
- Material explanations should make homeowners feel smart, not overwhelmed
- "When NOT to use this" sections build massive trust
- CTAs should feel like a natural next step, not a sales close

### Blog Posts
**Tone:** Educational, approachable, occasionally witty. Teaching mode.
- Open with the reader's situation, not our credentials
- Use subheadings as mini-promises ("Here's when to actually worry")
- End with clear next steps, not vague "contact us" platitudes

### Location Pages
**Tone:** Local, knowledgeable, community-aware. We know this area.
- Reference local landmarks, soil conditions, housing stock
- Feel like we've worked in this specific neighborhood
- Local knowledge = trust signal

### Team Bios
**Tone:** Human, warm, specific. Real people, not corporate headshots.
- Lead with what makes each person good at their job
- Personal details should feel genuine, not HR-approved
- Specialties should show depth, not just titles
