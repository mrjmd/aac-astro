## The Repair You Have Been Putting Off

You have a crack in your foundation. You know it needs fixing. But you have been putting it off because "foundation repair" sounds expensive and invasive — heavy equipment, your basement torn apart, maybe your house on jacks.

Take a breath. For the vast majority of foundation cracks in New England homes, the reality is nothing like that. A typical crack injection takes a few hours, involves no heavy equipment, creates minimal mess, and you can use your basement the same day.

Here is what a typical repair day actually looks like.

## The Scenario: A Typical Poured Concrete Foundation Crack

The most common job we do: a vertical or diagonal crack in a poured concrete foundation wall. The homeowner noticed water during heavy rain — a wet streak down the wall ending in a puddle on the floor. The crack might be thin as a pencil line or wide as a credit card edge. The house is usually 20 to 60 years old, and the crack is from normal shrinkage or minor settlement. For context on crack types, see our post on [vertical vs. horizontal foundation cracks](/blog/vertical-vs-horizontal-foundation-cracks).

## Step 1: Assessment (15-20 Minutes)

We trace the full length of the crack, checking width at multiple points. We look for signs of structural movement: is one side offset from the other? Is it wider at the top than the bottom? We check for previous repair attempts — old paint, caulk, or hydraulic cement covering the crack.

We also look at the bigger picture. Other cracks nearby? Is the wall bowing? Horizontal cracks indicating lateral pressure? We want to understand what caused the crack, not just treat the symptom.

After the assessment, we explain everything in plain English — what type of crack, what caused it, what the repair involves. If we think you need a structural engineer rather than injection, we say so. This assessment is part of our [free consultation](/services/free-foundation-consultations). You pay nothing unless you decide to proceed.

## Step 2: Preparation (15-20 Minutes)

We clean the crack surface to remove loose material, dirt, efflorescence, or previous repair attempts. We need clean concrete on both sides for proper bonding.

Next, we drill small holes along the crack every 6 to 12 inches, angling into the crack channel at about 45 degrees. Into each hole goes an injection port — a small fitting that serves as the entry point for repair material.

Between the ports, we apply an epoxy surface paste over the visible crack. This seals the interior face so injected material is forced through the full wall thickness rather than leaking out the front. The surface seal needs time to set — typically 20 minutes to an hour depending on product and temperature.

## Step 3: The Injection (30-60 Minutes)

This is the main event. Depending on crack type and conditions, we inject either epoxy resin (rigid, structural-strength, bonds the concrete back together) or polyurethane foam (flexible, expands slightly, better for cracks with minor movement). Both are injected under controlled pressure — up to 100 PSI for stubborn cracks, typically 20 to 40 PSI for standard work — ensuring the material fills the crack through the full 8-to-10 inches of concrete wall thickness.

We start at the bottom port and work up. We pump material into the first port and watch the next one up — when material appears there, we know the section between them is filled. Cap the current port, move to the next, repeat. This methodical approach ensures complete fill with no gaps.

The process is not loud. You will hear the quiet hum of injection equipment, possibly slight hissing. No hammering, grinding, or jackhammering. You can have a conversation at normal volume while we work.

## Step 4: Cleanup and Finishing (15-30 Minutes)

Once the material has set, we trim the injection ports flush with the wall surface and remove the surface seal paste residue. The finished result: a thin line of cured repair material where the crack was, plus small port patches every 6 to 12 inches. Not invisible, but clean and unobtrusive.

We pick up all materials, tools, and debris. Your basement should look the same as when we arrived — minus the crack.

## The Warranty and What to Expect After

Every crack injection comes with a lifetime warranty. If the repaired crack ever leaks again, we fix it at no charge. The warranty transfers with the house if you sell.

The repair is waterproof immediately. Full material cure takes 24 to 72 hours depending on product and temperature. No restrictions on using your basement — you can go back to normal right away.

## Total Time on Site

For a single crack: roughly 2 to 4 hours from arrival to departure. Two cracks might take 3 to 5 hours. We have done three-crack jobs in a half day. Compare that to what most people imagine when they hear "foundation repair."

## Beyond the Basic Crack

While the single vertical crack is our bread and butter, we handle plenty of other scenarios with similar techniques.

**Multiple cracks:** Assessed individually, often injected in a single visit.

**Previously failed repairs:** We regularly fix cracks that someone else attempted with hydraulic cement, caulk, or paint. Surface patches fail because they do not fill through the full wall thickness. We remove the old material and inject correctly.

**Wall cracks:** Our [wall crack repair service](/services/wall-crack-repair) covers variations in access and preparation for above-grade cracks.

**Bulkhead leaks:** [Leaky bulkheads](/services/leaky-bulkhead-repair) need tailored approaches using professional bulkhead sealant for the connection between the bulkhead structure and foundation wall. Most bulkhead repairs run $1,800-$2,500.

**Carbon fiber reinforcement:** For walls showing structural stress, carbon fiber staples (also called carbon fiber stitches) bridge cracks at $200-$300 per stitch, preventing further movement without the bulk of steel I-beams.
