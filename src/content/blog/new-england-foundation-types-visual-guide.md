## How to Identify Your Foundation Type

"Concrete, I think?" is the answer we hear most often during our [free consultations](/services/free-foundation-consultations). That is a perfectly reasonable answer — until something goes wrong and the repair approach depends entirely on what you actually have.

Here is the thing about New England: we have every foundation type imaginable, sometimes on the same street. A 1780 farmhouse with fieldstone walls sitting next to a 1960s ranch with poured concrete. Your repair options, costs, and urgency all depend on which type is holding up your house.

Head downstairs with a flashlight. Let us figure out what you are working with.

## Poured Concrete (Post-1940s)

The most common foundation type in New England by a wide margin. If your home was built after about 1940, this is almost certainly what you have.

**What it looks like:** Smooth, uniform gray walls. You might see faint horizontal lines where concrete forms were stacked during construction and small evenly-spaced holes (tie holes) where metal ties held the forms together. Walls are typically 8-10 inches of concrete and feel solid when you knock.

**Common problems:** Poured concrete cracks — that is physics, not a defect. Vertical or slightly diagonal cracks from shrinkage and settlement are the most common and most straightforward to repair. Horizontal cracks indicate lateral soil pressure and need prompt attention.

**Repair approach:** The easiest foundation type to fix. Cracks can be sealed permanently with [foundation crack injection](/services/foundation-crack-injection) at $800-$1,300 per crack, filling the crack from front to back with epoxy or polyurethane resin at approximately 100 PSI. See our [poured concrete foundation repair guide](/blog/poured-concrete-foundation-repair) for the full picture.

## Cinderblock / CMU (1930s-1970s)

Popular from the 1930s through the 1970s because they were fast to build without formwork. Common throughout Connecticut, Massachusetts, and the rest of New England in mid-century homes.

**What it looks like:** A grid of rectangular blocks with visible mortar joints. Standard blocks are 8 by 8 by 16 inches. The surface has a slightly rough, pebbled texture. Some block foundations were parged (coated with mortar) on the interior, making them look like poured concrete — knock on the wall, and if it sounds hollow, it is block.

**Common problems:** Instead of clean vertical cracks, you get stair-step patterns following the mortar joints. The hollow cores create a sneaky issue: water can enter at the top of the wall, fill the cores, and seep out at the bottom, making it look like a floor-level problem when the entry point is much higher.

**Repair approach:** Block foundations require different strategies than poured concrete. Repairs might involve injecting the cores, [repairing wall cracks](/services/wall-crack-repair), or addressing mortar joints. Our [cinderblock foundation repair guide](/blog/cinderblock-foundation-repair-guide) covers the details.

## Fieldstone (Pre-1900)

If your New England home was built before 1900, there is a good chance it sits on fieldstone — the same rocks that New England farmers spent centuries pulling out of their fields.

**What it looks like:** Unmistakable. Irregularly shaped stones of varying sizes fitted together like a three-dimensional puzzle, with mortar (usually a sandy lime-based mix) filling the gaps. Walls are often 18 to 24 inches thick. Colors vary by local geology: gray granite in Connecticut and Massachusetts, reddish sandstone in the Connecticut River Valley.

**Common problems:** Fieldstone foundations were never waterproof — they were not designed to be. The original builders expected some moisture and used cellars for cold storage. Mortar deteriorates over time, stones can shift, and water passes through freely during heavy rains. Efflorescence — that white powder on basement walls — is common and signals ongoing moisture movement.

**A note on cement vs. concrete:** Homeowners often say "cement foundation." Cement is just one ingredient in concrete. Fieldstone foundations use neither — they are natural stone and lime mortar. The distinction matters because repair materials differ completely.

**Repair approach:** Specialized work involving repointing mortar joints, stabilizing shifted stones, and managing water rather than eliminating it entirely. Our [fieldstone foundation repair guide](/blog/fieldstone-foundation-repair-guide) covers the specific approaches.

## Brick (1800s-1930s)

The standard for well-built homes during the industrial era. Walk through the older neighborhoods of Hartford, Worcester, or Providence and you will find them everywhere.

**What it looks like:** Fired clay bricks in regular courses with mortar joints. Usually deep red or reddish-brown, though you might see orange or yellow bricks depending on clay source. Walls are two or three bricks thick (8 to 12 inches). Older foundations use softer, hand-formed bricks; later ones use harder, machine-made bricks.

**Common problems:** The mortar fails before the bricks — almost always. Original lime mortar erodes from moisture, freeze-thaw cycles, and age. You will see crumbling mortar, recessed joints, and sometimes bricks you can wiggle free by hand. Brick spalling (face flaking off) is another common issue from moisture freezing inside the brick.

**Repair approach:** Mortar repointing is the primary repair, with a critical caveat: you must use lime-based mortar, not Portland cement, on pre-1920 foundations. Portland cement is too hard and will damage the bricks. We cover this in detail in our [brick foundation repair guide](/blog/brick-foundation-repair-guide).

## Rubble Stone and Granite (1700s-1800s)

The oldest foundations in New England — under colonial homes, early Federal-era houses, and barns that have somehow survived three centuries of our weather.

**What it looks like:** Rubble stone uses rough, minimally shaped stones with wide mortar joints and irregular placement. Cut granite features large, precisely shaped blocks with thin mortar joints — the premium option of their day.

**Common problems:** Rubble stone shares fieldstone's issues: mortar deterioration, stone shifting, water intrusion. The mortar may be the original 200-plus-year-old lime-and-sand mix, largely gone in places. Cut granite holds up better structurally, but mortar joints still fail. The biggest concern is whether the foundation is still doing its job structurally after centuries of soil pressure.

**Repair approach:** These foundations need careful repair matching original materials and techniques. Using modern Portland cement on a 250-year-old rubble stone wall can trap moisture and cause more damage.
