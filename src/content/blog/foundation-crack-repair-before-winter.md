## The Freeze-Thaw Problem

There is a specific kind of procrastination that New England homeowners are very good at. You notice a foundation crack in spring. You make a mental note. Summer comes and goes — the crack is not leaking, so it falls off your radar. Fall arrives, but you are busy.

Then winter hits.

Here is what happens: water seeps into the crack during fall rains. The temperature drops below freezing. That water expands by approximately 9 percent, generating roughly 30,000 pounds per square inch of force — enough to split granite. The expanding ice widens the crack. When temperatures rise above freezing (which happens regularly — we do not get one long freeze, we get dozens of cycles), the ice melts. The wider crack accepts more water. Freezes again. Expands again.

This repeats 50 to 80 times in a typical New England winter. Each cycle ratchets the crack open a little further, never clicking back. By spring, that hairline crack might be three or four times wider — meaning more water, potential mold, and a more expensive repair.

## What One Winter Does to Different Crack Types

**Vertical shrinkage cracks:** The most common type in poured concrete. Typically hairline width and structurally insignificant, but freeze-thaw does not care about significance. By spring these can be one-eighth inch or wider. These are also the easiest and cheapest to repair — a single [crack injection](/services/foundation-crack-injection) costs a fraction of what you will pay after winter damage.

**Diagonal settlement cracks:** Usually wider at one end. Freeze-thaw accelerates the wider end, and differential movement can increase.

**Horizontal cracks:** Indicate lateral soil pressure. Winter makes this worse two ways: freeze-thaw widens the crack AND frozen soil expands against the wall, increasing pressure. Getting these assessed before winter is especially important. See our guide on [vertical vs. horizontal foundation cracks](/blog/vertical-vs-horizontal-foundation-cracks).

**Stair-step cracks in block foundations:** Follow mortar joints in a zigzag. Freeze-thaw deteriorates mortar and widens joints. Our [cinderblock foundation repair guide](/blog/cinderblock-foundation-repair-guide) covers the specific challenges.

### Spring Repair: Bigger Problem, Higher Price

After a winter of freeze-thaw, the same crack might need more material (wider crack, possible branching), additional surface prep for ice-damaged concrete, and possibly [wall crack repair](/services/wall-crack-repair) techniques beyond simple injection. The repair itself can cost 50 to 100 percent more.

### The Hidden Costs

Water through a widened crack over winter can mean mold growth ($1,500 to $5,000 for remediation), damaged stored belongings (usually not covered by insurance), efflorescence and staining, and in rare cases, structural escalation requiring carbon fiber reinforcement.

## Why Fall Is the Ideal Repair Window

**Material performance:** Injection resins cure best when concrete temperatures are above 40 degrees Fahrenheit. In September and October, foundation walls are still warm from summer. By December, unheated basements may drop below the effective range.

**Access and conditions:** Ground is not frozen, landscaping is accessible, basements are comfortable. In spring, we compete with snowmelt, saturated soil, and sometimes standing water.

**Scheduling:** Spring is busier because it includes emergency calls from homeowners who did not fix things in fall. Fall lead times are shorter and more predictable.

**Peace of mind:** Knowing your foundation is sealed going into winter eliminates the background anxiety of wondering what is happening during every cold snap.

## What About Temporary Fixes?

Can you slap hydraulic cement on the crack to get through winter? Technically, but it is a band-aid on a wound that needs stitches.

Hydraulic cement and caulk only seal the interior surface. The crack extends through the full 8-to-10-inch wall. Water still enters from the exterior, fills the crack behind the patch, and freezes. The ice pushes the patch off from behind, usually within one to three freeze-thaw cycles. Worse, the patch can trap water inside the wall, concentrating damage where you cannot see it.

If you truly cannot get a professional repair done before winter, the better temporary measure is to address the water source: improve grading, extend downspouts, and direct water away from the cracked wall. Less water reaching the crack means less freeze-thaw damage. Our [fall foundation inspection checklist](/blog/fall-foundation-inspection-checklist) covers the steps.

## Your Foundation Is Not Getting Younger

No foundation crack heals itself. Concrete does not regenerate. Cracks either stay the same (if you are lucky) or get worse (if you live in New England, which you do). Every winter is another cycle of damage to unrepaired cracks.

The best time to fix a foundation crack was when it first appeared. The second-best time is right now, before winter.
