## The Internet Is Full of Foundation Advice. Most of It Is Wrong.

If you have ever Googled "foundation crack" at 11 PM after noticing something in your basement, you know how this goes. Within five minutes, you have read three forum posts saying your house is about to collapse, two articles saying it is nothing, and one Reddit thread recommending a repair method that involves flex tape and prayer.

After 33 years of repairing foundations across New England — Connecticut, Massachusetts, Rhode Island, New Hampshire, and Maine — we have encountered every myth, misconception, and half-truth about foundation repair that exists. Some of these myths are just annoying. Others actually cost homeowners money by delaying necessary repairs or leading them toward solutions that do not work.

Let us set the record straight.

## Myth 1: "Foundation Repair Costs $50,000+"

This is the big one. The fear that foundation repair requires a second mortgage keeps people from even getting an assessment.

**The reality:** Most repairs we perform cost between $500 and $3,000. A typical [crack injection](/services/foundation-crack-injection) runs $800 to $1,500. Even [carbon fiber reinforcement](/services/carbon-fiber-stitches) or [bulkhead repair](/services/leaky-bulkhead-repair) usually falls in the $1,500 to $4,000 range.

Can foundation work cost $50,000? Sure — if you need to underpin an entire house or address severe structural failure. But those situations are rare. The vast majority of issues we see are cracks, minor water intrusion, and deteriorated bulkheads. Check our [foundation repair cost guide](/blog/foundation-repair-cost-guide-2026) for real numbers.

## Myth 2: "A Crack Means Your House Is Falling Down"

You see a crack, you imagine the worst, and suddenly you are pricing condos.

**The reality:** Concrete cracks. That is what concrete does. Every poured concrete foundation in New England has cracks. Every single one. The question is not whether cracks exist — it is what kind they are and whether they are changing.

**Vertical cracks** are usually shrinkage cracks — normal, fixable, not structural emergencies. They should be sealed to prevent water intrusion, especially where freeze-thaw can widen them. **Horizontal cracks** can indicate lateral soil pressure and should be evaluated, but are usually manageable with [wall repair](/services/wall-crack-repair). **Stair-step cracks** in block foundations can indicate settling — worth evaluating, rarely catastrophic.

A crack is information, not a death sentence.

## Myth 3: "DIY Epoxy From the Hardware Store Works Fine"

Walk into any home improvement store and you will find shelves of crack repair products. Tubes of caulk, cans of hydraulic cement, epoxy kits with optimistic instructions. They are cheap and the packaging makes it look easy.

**The reality:** A typical foundation wall is 8 to 10 inches thick. A tube of caulk seals maybe 1/4 inch of that. The remaining 7+ inches of crack is still open, still allowing water through, and still vulnerable to freeze-thaw damage.

Professional [crack injection](/services/foundation-crack-injection) fills the crack from front to back — the entire depth of the wall. We have lost count of how many times we have been called to re-repair a crack that a homeowner tried to fix with hardware store products. The DIY fix lasted a season, then the crack opened up next to the patch. The homeowner ended up paying for both the failed attempt and the professional repair.

We wrote an entire article about this: [DIY vs. professional foundation repair](/blog/diy-vs-professional-foundation-repair). Some repairs need to be done from the inside out, and that requires professional equipment and materials.

## Myth 4: "Foundation Problems Mean You Cannot Sell Your House"

This myth has two victims: sellers who panic and buyers who walk away from perfectly good homes.

**The reality:** Homes with documented, repaired foundation issues sell every day. What tanks a home sale is not foundation repair — it is foundation mystery. ## Myth 5: "All Cracks Are the Same"

**The reality:** Cracks are as varied as the foundations they appear in. A quick primer:

- **Hairline vertical cracks** — almost always normal shrinkage. Monitor and seal.
- **Wider vertical cracks** (over 1/8 inch) — may indicate settling. Get an evaluation.
- **Horizontal cracks** — lateral soil pressure. These need attention.
- **Diagonal cracks** near corners — differential settling.
- **Cracks with displacement** (one side higher than the other) — the most serious. Call a professional.
- **Cracks that are growing** — the cause is ongoing.

The location, direction, width, and behavior of a crack all tell a different story. Treating all cracks the same is like treating all coughs the same — sometimes it is a cold, sometimes it needs a doctor.

## Myth 6: "You Need a Full Perimeter Drain System"

Some contractors push expensive drainage systems for every foundation issue. It is the foundation repair equivalent of recommending a full engine rebuild when you need an oil change.

**The reality:** Perimeter drain systems are legitimate solutions for widespread water intrusion from high water tables. If your entire basement floor is weeping water, a drainage system might be appropriate.

But for a leaking crack or a damp bulkhead, it is overkill. [Crack injection](/services/foundation-crack-injection) seals the entry point directly. [Bulkhead repair](/services/leaky-bulkhead-repair) addresses the bulkhead specifically. Targeted repairs solve targeted problems at a fraction of the cost.

We have seen homeowners quoted $15,000 for drainage systems when their actual problem was a single crack that could have been injected for $800. Always get a second opinion.

## Myth 7: "Insurance Always Covers Foundation Repair"

**The reality:** Standard homeowners policies typically do not cover foundation repair caused by settling, shrinkage, or hydrostatic pressure. These are considered maintenance issues, not covered events.

Insurance may cover damage from a specific covered event — a burst pipe, a vehicle impact, or sudden accidental water discharge. But the key words are "sudden and accidental." Gradual water intrusion through a developing crack will not be covered. Our guide on [insurance coverage for foundation repair](/blog/does-insurance-cover-foundation-repair) has the full breakdown.
