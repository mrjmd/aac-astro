## Why Garage Floors Crack in New England

If you have a garage in New England, you have garage floor cracks. That is not pessimism — it is concrete science combined with a climate famous for frost heave, clay soils, and temperature swings from negative 10 to 95 degrees Fahrenheit.

Not all garage floor cracks are created equal. Some are completely normal. Others are telling you something important about what is happening underneath. The trick is knowing which is which.

## The Physics of Cracking Concrete

Concrete is going to crack. It is not a matter of if but when and where.

**Shrinkage:** When concrete cures, it loses moisture and shrinks. A 20-foot slab can shrink about half an inch. Good contractors cut control joints — deliberate grooves where the concrete will crack in a controlled line rather than randomly. When it works, the cracks hide inside the joints.

**Frost heave:** The New England special. Soil beneath unheated garage slabs freezes, expands, and pushes the slab upward. It settles back down when it thaws — but not always evenly. Years of this cycle create upheaval and progressive cracking.

**Settlement:** Soil disturbed during construction compacts unevenly over time, creating voids beneath the slab. The rigid concrete cracks where it loses support.

**Heavy loads:** Your car sits on the same spots every day, concentrating 3,000 to 5,000 pounds on a slab that is typically only 4 inches thick.

## Types of Cracks: Cosmetic vs. Concerning

**Hairline surface cracks** (under one-sixteenth of an inch): Cosmetic shrinkage cracks. Fill them if they bother you, or ignore them.

**Control joint cracks:** The joint did its job. Expected and harmless.

**Map cracking** (network of shallow cracks like a dried riverbed): The surface dried too fast during curing. Ugly but not dangerous.

**Straight cracks across the slab:** Usually shrinkage that found its own path. Generally cosmetic unless widening over time.

**Cracks with vertical offset** (one side higher than the other): Pay attention. Something is moving beneath the slab — settlement or frost heave. This is structural.

**Wide or growing cracks:** If you can fit a quarter in it, or it is getting wider each season, the slab is actively moving. You need to understand why before you can fix it.

**Cracks with water seeping through:** High water table or surface water finding its way under the garage. Needs attention because that water will freeze in winter and accelerate everything.

## DIY Repair Options

For cosmetic cracks — hairlines, stable surface cracks, cracks inside control joints — DIY is perfectly reasonable.

**Concrete crack filler:** For narrow cracks under a quarter inch. Clean the crack, blow out dust, apply filler. Not invisible, but keeps water and debris out.

**Epoxy or polyurea kits:** For slightly wider cracks. Two-part products that bond to concrete. Polyurea is the better choice for garages because it stays flexible — important in a slab that moves with temperature.

**Concrete resurfacer:** For widespread surface cracking. A thin coating (about one-eighth inch) over the entire floor creates a new smooth surface. Does not fix structural issues but refreshes the appearance.

## When You Need Professional Help

**Active settlement or heaving:** If slab sections are sinking, rising, or tilting, the problem is beneath the slab. Professional options include mudjacking (pumping slurry beneath the slab) or polyurethane foam injection to lift settled sections and fill voids.

**Cracks connecting to foundation walls:** If a garage floor crack extends into a foundation wall, you may have a foundation issue. This is especially true if the wall crack shows widening or water intrusion — the foundation crack needs [assessment and potential injection](/services/foundation-crack-injection). **Widespread deterioration:** If the slab is crumbling or badly heaved in multiple spots, a new slab may be most cost-effective. Typically $3,000 to $6,000 for a two-car garage.

## Preventing Future Damage

**Manage water:** Slope the ground away from the garage. Extend downspouts at least 4 feet from the foundation.

**Seal the concrete:** A penetrating sealer every two to three years reduces freeze-thaw damage. Inexpensive insurance.

**Skip the salt:** De-icing salt accelerates concrete deterioration. Sweep or shovel snow instead. If you must de-ice, use sand or calcium magnesium acetate.

**Fix drainage early:** Water pooling against the garage foundation or on the floor gets worse fast. Our [fall inspection checklist](/blog/fall-foundation-inspection-checklist) covers the drainage steps.

Most garage floor cracks in New England are cosmetic — the natural consequence of concrete in a harsh climate. A tube of crack filler and attention to drainage handle the majority of situations.
