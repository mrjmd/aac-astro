## Closing the Books on Your Foundation's Year

December is when everyone does their annual review — finances, goals, that gym membership you definitely used. But there is one annual review that most homeowners skip, and it might be the most important one: your foundation.

Your home's foundation has been quietly doing its job all year long, handling spring snowmelt, summer humidity, fall rains, and the onset of winter frost. It has been pushed, pulled, soaked, and frozen. Now is the time to take stock of how it handled all that, document anything worth noting, and plan for the year ahead.

This is not about creating anxiety. Most foundations in New England are doing just fine. This is about being organized and proactive so that small issues stay small and big issues get caught before they become expensive.

Think of it as a year-end performance review for the most important structural element of your home. Let us get into it.

## Step 1: Review the Year's Events

Before you even head down to the basement, sit down for five minutes and think about what happened this year that might have affected your foundation.

### Weather Events

Did your area experience any unusual weather? Heavy rainfall events, extended dry spells, early or late frost, ice storms — all of these affect foundations. New England in particular throws curveballs. A drought followed by heavy rain causes soil to shrink and then rapidly expand, creating stress on foundation walls. An [ice dam season](/blog/ice-dam-foundation-damage) can redirect water in ways that damage foundations from unexpected angles.

### Home Changes

Did you add a patio, change your landscaping, install a new gutter system, or alter the grading around your home? Did you have a tree removed? Any change to how water moves around your property can affect your foundation, sometimes positively, sometimes not.

### Past Observations

If you have been following our advice (like the [Thanksgiving basement check](/blog/thanksgiving-basement-check)), pull up any photos or notes you made throughout the year. This is where documentation pays off — you can see trends that a single snapshot would miss.

## Step 2: Do a Thorough Basement Walk-Through

Now head downstairs with your phone camera, a flashlight, and a notepad (or a notes app — we are not picky about your documentation method, just that you do it).

### Walls

Walk the full perimeter of your basement, examining every wall. You are looking for:

- **New cracks** that were not there earlier in the year
- **Existing cracks that have changed** — wider, longer, or showing displacement
- **Water stains** — dark marks, mineral deposits (white efflorescence), or tide lines
- **Bowing or bulging** — stand at one end of each wall and sight down its length. Does it look straight?
- **Mortar deterioration** in block or stone foundations

### Floor

Walk the entire floor, especially where it meets the walls (the cove joint). Look for:

- Cracks, especially those with moisture or staining
- Uneven areas or sections that seem to have sunk
- Evidence of water pooling

### Mechanical Systems

Check your sump pump (if you have one) by pouring water into the pit and verifying it activates and drains properly. Check your dehumidifier — is it running, is the drain clear, is it keeping up with moisture levels?

### Bulkhead and Entry Points

Inspect your [bulkhead](/services/leaky-bulkhead-repair) for new deterioration, failed seals, or evidence of water entry. Check around windows, pipe penetrations, and any other points where things pass through your foundation walls.

## Step 3: Document Everything

This is the step most people skip, and it is the one that makes the biggest difference. Proper documentation turns vague worry into actionable information.

### Take Dated Photos

Photograph every crack, stain, or area of concern. Include something for scale — a ruler or coin next to a crack. Your phone timestamps photos automatically, and a crack photographed on three different dates tells us whether it is growing, stable, or seasonal.

### Measure and Organize

For cracks you are monitoring, measure the width at the widest point. A crack that was 1/16 inch in March and 1/8 inch in December is actively moving and needs professional evaluation. Start a dedicated folder on your phone labeled by location (e.g., "north_wall_corner_crack_dec2026.jpg").

## Step 4: Plan for Spring

In New England, most foundation repair work happens in spring and early summer. The ground thaws, the repair materials cure properly, and homeowners who planned ahead get on the schedule before the rush.

### Prioritize What You Found

Using your walk-through notes, categorize issues:

**Schedule now for spring:** Active water intrusion, cracks that have grown during the year, horizontal wall cracks, failed sump pump, or a bulkhead that leaks significantly. These should not wait. Call us in January to get on the spring schedule — the early spots fill fast.

**Monitor through winter:** Stable cracks, minor efflorescence, slight humidity. Check these monthly through the winter and reassess in March.

**No action needed:** Hairline cracks that have not changed, normal condensation on cold pipes, minor cosmetic issues. These are normal for New England homes.

## Step 5: Set Up a Monitoring Routine for Next Year

The best time to start a monitoring routine was when you bought your house. The second best time is right now. Here is a simple quarterly schedule that takes about 15 minutes each time:

### January (Winter Check)

Look for frost heave evidence around the exterior, check for ice dams on the roof, and scan the basement for new moisture.

### April (Spring Thaw Check)

The most important check of the year. Spring thaw delivers massive water to your foundation quickly. Inspect for new stains, test your sump pump, and verify grading. Our [spring thaw foundation prep guide](/blog/spring-thaw-foundation-prep) covers this in detail.

### July (Summer Check)

The driest season makes a good baseline. Check humidity, note any cracks that have closed (seasonal movement indicator), and schedule non-urgent repairs.

### October (Fall Check)

Your last chance to seal cracks before freeze-thaw season. Our [fall foundation inspection checklist](/blog/fall-foundation-inspection-checklist) is designed for this check.
