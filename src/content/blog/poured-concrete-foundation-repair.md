If your New England home was built after about 1940, there's a very good chance you're sitting on a poured concrete foundation. It's the most common foundation type in our region, and after 20 years of repairing them across five states, we have some good news: poured concrete is actually the best foundation type to repair. Not the cheapest to build originally, but when something goes wrong, the fixes are reliable, permanent, and typically less expensive than repairs on other foundation types.

Here's everything you need to know about your poured concrete foundation — what makes it crack, what those cracks mean, and when to actually pick up the phone.

## What Is a Poured Concrete Foundation?

A poured concrete foundation is exactly what it sounds like: liquid concrete poured into forms set on a footing, then allowed to cure into solid walls. Typical residential walls are 8-10 inches thick, though older pours might be as thin as 6 inches and commercial or custom builds can go up to 12 inches.

The construction process involves setting wooden or metal forms around the perimeter of the footings, placing rebar for reinforcement, and pouring the concrete in a continuous operation. Once cured, the forms are stripped, and you've got a monolithic wall — a single, continuous piece of concrete with no mortar joints or individual blocks.

This monolithic nature is exactly why poured concrete is the easiest foundation type to repair. Unlike [cinderblock foundations](/blog/cinderblock-foundation-repair-guide) where cracks follow mortar joints and water can enter through hollow cores, or [fieldstone foundations](/blog/fieldstone-foundation-repair-guide) where irregular surfaces make sealing difficult, a poured wall gives you a single uniform material to work with.

## Why Poured Concrete Cracks

Here's a truth that surprises many homeowners: **every poured concrete foundation will crack.** Not might — will. Concrete shrinks as it cures, and that shrinkage creates internal stresses that eventually relieve themselves through cracking. It's physics, not poor construction.

Understanding why your wall cracked helps determine the right repair:

### Curing Shrinkage

As concrete cures over the first months and years, it loses moisture and contracts. This creates shrinkage cracks — typically vertical or slightly diagonal, starting from stress points like window corners, pipe penetrations, or the middle of long walls. These are the most common cracks we see across New England, and they're almost always non-structural.

### Hydrostatic Pressure

Water-saturated soil pushes against your foundation walls with surprising force. In New England, spring snowmelt combined with heavy rain can create enormous hydrostatic pressure. This typically causes vertical cracks that may weep water, or in severe cases, horizontal cracking in the lower third of the wall.

### Soil Settlement

The ground under and around your foundation isn't static. Clay-heavy soils (common in the Connecticut River Valley and parts of eastern Massachusetts) expand when wet and contract when dry. Over years, this can create differential settlement that produces diagonal cracks, especially at corners.

### Freeze-Thaw Cycling

This is New England's specialty. Water enters tiny pores in the concrete, freezes, expands by 9%, then thaws. Repeat this a hundred times a winter for fifty winters, and you've got surface deterioration and potential cracking. Vermont and New Hampshire foundations take an especially brutal beating from freeze-thaw.

### Tree Roots

That beautiful maple in your backyard? Its root system may extend far beyond its canopy, and roots seeking moisture can exert significant pressure on foundation walls. We see this frequently in the older, tree-lined neighborhoods of towns like West Hartford, Newton, and Providence.

## Common Crack Patterns and What They Mean

Not all cracks are created equal. Here's how to read what your foundation is telling you. For a deeper dive, see our complete guide on [vertical vs. horizontal foundation cracks](/blog/vertical-vs-horizontal-foundation-cracks).

### Vertical Cracks

**What they look like:** Running roughly straight up and down, often from the top of the wall toward (but not always reaching) the bottom.

**What they mean:** Almost always shrinkage cracks. These are the most common cracks in poured concrete and typically the least concerning structurally. However, they're the number-one entry point for water in basements.

**Repair approach:** [Foundation crack injection](/services/foundation-crack-injection) is the standard repair — and it's ideal for poured concrete because the crack goes straight through the wall, giving injected material a clean path to fill the entire void.

### Diagonal Cracks

**What they look like:** Running at roughly 30-60 degrees from horizontal, often from corners of windows or where walls meet.

**What they mean:** Usually differential settlement. One part of the foundation is moving differently than another. Worth monitoring for progression, but generally repairable without major structural intervention.

**Repair approach:** Injection for waterproofing, sometimes combined with [carbon fiber stitches](/services/carbon-fiber-stitches) for added structural reinforcement if movement is ongoing.

### Horizontal Cracks

**What they look like:** Running roughly level, typically in the lower to middle third of the wall.

**What they mean:** Lateral soil pressure is pushing the wall inward. This is the most structurally significant crack pattern in poured concrete. If you see horizontal cracking, especially with any inward bowing, call a professional promptly.

**Repair approach:** Depending on severity, this may require [wall crack repair](/services/wall-crack-repair) with carbon fiber reinforcement, steel I-beams, or wall anchors. The sooner you address it, the less invasive the repair.

## Repair Methods for Poured Concrete

Poured concrete's biggest repair advantage is accessibility. Because the wall is a single monolithic piece, repair materials can penetrate through the full thickness.

### Crack Injection

This is the workhorse of poured concrete repair. We drill injection ports along the crack, seal the surface, and inject either polyurethane foam or structural epoxy under pressure. The material follows the crack from the interior surface through the full wall thickness to the soil side.

**Polyurethane** stays flexible, accommodating minor seasonal movement. Ideal for shrinkage cracks that may shift slightly with temperature changes.

**Epoxy** cures rigid and actually bonds the crack faces together, creating a repair that's stronger than the surrounding concrete. Better for structural applications.

Either way, the result is a permanently sealed crack. Our [foundation crack injection](/services/foundation-crack-injection) page covers the process in detail.

### Carbon Fiber Reinforcement

For cracks showing structural movement, we combine injection with [carbon fiber stitches](/services/carbon-fiber-stitches). The injection seals the crack; the carbon fiber prevents it from reopening. The smooth, uniform surface of poured concrete makes it ideal for carbon fiber adhesion — another advantage over block or stone foundations.

### Exterior Waterproofing

In cases where interior injection alone isn't sufficient — say, a wall with multiple cracks and heavy water pressure — exterior waterproofing may be recommended. This involves excavating along the wall, applying waterproof membrane, and improving drainage. It's more invasive and expensive but addresses the water problem at its source.

## Poured Concrete vs. Other Foundation Types

Wondering how your poured concrete foundation stacks up? Here's the honest comparison:

| Feature | Poured Concrete | Cinderblock | Fieldstone |
|---------|----------------|-------------|------------|
| Crack repairability | Excellent | Moderate | Difficult |
| Water resistance | Good (monolithic) | Poor (hollow cores) | Poor (irregular joints) |
| Structural strength | High | Moderate | Variable |
| Typical repair cost | Lower | Moderate | Higher |
| Expected lifespan | 100+ years | 80-100 years | Indefinite if maintained |

If you have a cinderblock or fieldstone foundation, don't panic — they're absolutely repairable too. Check out our [cinderblock foundation repair guide](/blog/cinderblock-foundation-repair-guide) and [fieldstone foundation repair guide](/blog/fieldstone-foundation-repair-guide) for specifics.

## When to Worry (And When Not To)

After looking at thousands of poured concrete foundations across Connecticut, Massachusetts, Rhode Island, New Hampshire, and Maine, here's our honest take on when to worry:

**Don't lose sleep over:**
- Hairline shrinkage cracks with no water entry
- Single vertical cracks that haven't changed in years
- Minor efflorescence (white mineral deposits) around cracks

**Get an inspection soon:**
- Any crack actively leaking water
- Cracks wider than 1/4 inch
- Multiple new cracks appearing in a short time
- Cracks near window wells or utility penetrations

**Call us today:**
- Horizontal cracking at any width
- Visible wall bowing or leaning
- Cracks with displacement (one side higher or further out than the other)
- Water pooling in the basement during rain

For anything on that last list, the [DIY approach](/blog/diy-vs-professional-foundation-repair) isn't going to cut it. You need a professional assessment.
