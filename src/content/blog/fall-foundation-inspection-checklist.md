## Why Fall Is the Most Important Season for Your Foundation

September through November is the window. After that, the ground freezes and whatever problems your foundation had going in become bigger problems by spring.

Water in foundation cracks freezes and expands by about 9 percent. That expansion forces the crack wider. When it thaws, the wider crack lets in more water. One New England winter can take a hairline crack and turn it into a quarter-inch gap. Our post on [foundation crack repair before winter](/blog/foundation-crack-repair-before-winter) explains the mechanics in detail.

A thorough fall inspection takes about an hour, costs nothing, and can save thousands in spring repairs. Grab a flashlight, a tape measure, and this checklist.

## Exterior Checklist

Walk the entire perimeter of your house on a dry day, examining the foundation from ground level.

### Grading and Slope

The ground should slope away from the house at about 1 inch per foot for the first 6 feet. Over time, soil settles and mulch compacts, flattening or reversing the grade. If water would pool against the foundation during rain, add topsoil to rebuild the slope. This $50 fix prevents $5,000 problems.

### Gutters and Downspouts

Clean gutters of leaves and debris. Check where downspouts discharge — if water exits at the base of the wall, add extensions to carry it at least 4 to 6 feet from the house. A typical roof dumps 600 gallons per 1,000 square feet during a 1-inch rain. That water is either going away from your house or directly against your foundation.

### Visible Foundation Cracks

Look for new cracks or existing ones that appear wider. White mineral deposits (efflorescence) or staining below a crack means water is actively moving through. Note locations, lengths, and widths — take photos with a coin for scale. For help evaluating what you find, see [how to check your foundation](/blog/how-to-check-your-foundation) and our guide to [vertical vs. horizontal cracks](/blog/vertical-vs-horizontal-foundation-cracks).

### Window Wells

Clean out leaves, debris, and standing water. If there is no drain at the bottom, consider a clear plastic cover ($20 to $40) to keep rain out while letting in light. Standing water in a window well presses directly against your foundation at a vulnerable point.

### Bulkhead / Hatchway

Check doors for rust, rot, or gaps. Check the bulkhead sealant where the frame meets the foundation wall. Look for cracks in the steps and walls. If your bulkhead leaks, our [leaky bulkhead repair service](/services/leaky-bulkhead-repair) ($1,800-$2,500) can address it before winter makes it worse — including proper bulkhead sealant application at all joints.

## Interior Checklist

Head inside with a good flashlight and walk the entire basement perimeter.

### Foundation Walls

**Cracks:** If you have been monitoring cracks (a pencil mark at the crack tip with a date is the simplest method), check for growth. Measure widths with a tape.

**Water signs:** Efflorescence (white powder on basement walls), damp spots, or staining mean moisture is moving through the wall. Note whether it corresponds to a visible crack.

**Bowing or leaning:** Hold a straight edge against the wall vertically. More than half an inch of inward lean warrants professional assessment.

### Floor Cracks

Check for cracks in the basement floor, especially around the perimeter where floor meets walls. Cracks showing water staining or vertical displacement deserve attention.

### Humidity

Basement humidity above 60 percent encourages mold and wood rot. A $10 hygrometer is invaluable for monitoring. If you have a dehumidifier, confirm it is working properly.

### Sump Pump Test

Pour a bucket of water into the sump pit until the float switch activates. Watch it cycle. Listen for unusual noises. Check that the discharge pipe outside is clear and not aimed back at the house. If you have a battery backup, check the battery — replace it if it is over two to three years old.

### Rim Joist Area

Where the wooden structure sits on the foundation wall is often poorly insulated and a source of cold air and moisture. Seal gaps with foam insulation or caulk.

## The Drainage Check

During the next moderate to heavy rain, put on a raincoat and walk around your house. Watch for water pooling against the foundation, downspout overflow, low spots directing water toward the house, or runoff from neighboring properties. Fifteen minutes of rain observation teaches more than an hour of dry inspection.

After the rain, check your basement for new moisture. Water appearing during rain likely enters through wall cracks. Water appearing hours later (or during snowmelt) may be groundwater pressure from below.

## Priority Action List

### Fix This Week
- Clean gutters and extend downspouts
- Clean window wells
- Fix grading problems with topsoil

### Fix This Month
- Seal visible cracks — for a permanent fix, [professional injection](/services/foundation-crack-injection) ($800-$1,200 per crack) is the way to go
- Repair leaky bulkhead seals with proper bulkhead sealant
- Test and service the sump pump
- Seal rim joist gaps

### Schedule Before Winter
- Professional assessment for structural concerns (bowing walls, growing cracks, significant water intrusion)
- Foundation crack injection for active leaks
- Bulkhead repair for persistent leaks
