Most homeowners think of spring and winter as foundation problem seasons — snowmelt flooding, freeze-thaw cracking, April showers overwhelming drainage. Summer gets a pass. You are barbecuing, not thinking about your basement.

But summer creates its own set of foundation stresses, and ignoring them means discovering new cracks when fall rain arrives. After 20 years of repairing foundations across New England, we can tell you that a surprising number of fall service calls trace back to summer conditions that nobody noticed.

## How Summer Heat Affects Your Foundation

### Clay-Heavy Soil Shrinkage and Settlement

This is the big one. During extended dry periods, clay-heavy soil and glacial till (common across much of New England) shrinks as it loses moisture. That shrinkage creates a gap between the soil and your foundation wall. When rain finally returns, water pours directly down this gap and hits your foundation at the footing — exactly where you do not want concentrated water flow.

Even worse, if the soil shrinks unevenly around your foundation, it can cause differential settlement. One corner of your house loses support while another stays put. The result: new cracks, sticking doors, and subtle shifts that may not be obvious until they become serious.

**What to watch for:**
- Visible gaps between soil and your foundation wall
- Cracks appearing in previously solid concrete
- Doors or windows that suddenly stick or will not close properly
- New cracks in interior drywall near floor level

### Thermal Expansion and Contraction

Concrete expands when hot and contracts when cool. During summer, the sun-facing side of your foundation heats up while the opposite side stays relatively cool. This differential temperature creates stress within the concrete. Existing hairline cracks can widen during hot spells and close during cool nights, a cycle that gradually works the crack larger over the course of the summer.

This is not going to collapse your house overnight. But if you have cracks you have been watching, check them during a heat wave. If they are visibly wider than they were in spring, the thermal cycling may be accelerating their growth.

### Basement Humidity

Summer air holds more moisture than winter air. When that warm, humid air enters your cooler basement, it condenses on foundation walls, pipes, and floors. This creates the damp, musty basement conditions that many New England homeowners accept as normal but should not.

Sustained high humidity does three things to your foundation:
1. Promotes mold and mildew growth on walls and stored items
2. Accelerates deterioration of mortar in older stone foundations
3. Masks actual water intrusion — you cannot tell the difference between condensation and a leaking crack when everything is damp

For a deeper dive, see our [basement humidity control guide](/blog/basement-humidity-control-guide).

## Your Summer Foundation Maintenance Checklist

### Check Your Grading

Walk the perimeter of your house and look at how the ground slopes away from the foundation. The soil should slope downward at least 6 inches over the first 10 feet from the foundation wall. If the grade is flat or slopes toward the house, rainwater will pool against your foundation.

Summer is the best time to fix grading because the soil is dry and easy to work with. Add topsoil against the foundation, slope it away, and compact it. This is one of the most effective and least expensive things you can do for your foundation.

### Inspect and Clean Gutters and Downspouts

Gutters clogged with spring pollen and seeds dump water right next to your foundation instead of directing it away. Make sure:
- Gutters are clear and flowing freely
- Downspouts extend at least 4 to 6 feet from the foundation
- Splash blocks or extensions are in place and aimed away from the house
- No downspouts are disconnected or dumping water at the foundation

### Monitor Foundation Cracks

If you know about existing cracks, summer is the time to track them. Mark the ends of any cracks with a pencil line and date it. Check monthly. If a crack grows past your mark, it is active and should be evaluated before winter.

New cracks that appear during summer drought often indicate soil shrinkage settlement. These should be inspected — a typical crack injection runs $800-$1,200 with a lifetime warranty if repair is needed. Not every crack is a crisis, but new cracks deserve professional assessment to determine if they are cosmetic or structural.

### When NOT to Worry About Summer Cracks

Hairline cracks (thinner than 1/16 inch) that appear during a heat wave and close back up when temperatures moderate are often thermal movement — the concrete expanding and contracting. Mark them and monitor through fall. If they stay stable and dry through the rainy season, they may not need repair at all.

For help distinguishing crack types, see our guide on [vertical vs. horizontal foundation cracks](/blog/vertical-vs-horizontal-foundation-cracks).

### Water Your Foundation (Yes, Really)

This sounds strange, but during extended dry spells, lightly watering the soil around your foundation can prevent the shrinkage gaps described above. You are not trying to soak the ground — just maintain consistent soil moisture so it does not pull away from the foundation wall.

Use a soaker hose placed 12 to 18 inches from the foundation, running it for 15 to 20 minutes every few days during drought conditions. The goal is moist soil, not mud.

This is particularly important for homes with clay soil and for older foundations that are more susceptible to settlement.

### Check Window Wells and Basement Windows

Window wells collect debris, leaves, and rainwater. Clear them out in early summer and again in mid-August. Make sure:
- Window well drains are functional (pour some water in and see if it drains)
- Basement windows close and seal properly
- Window well covers are in place if applicable
- No cracks have developed around the window frame in the foundation wall

### Control Basement Humidity

If your basement runs above 60% relative humidity in summer, take action:
- Run a dehumidifier set to 50% or lower
- Ensure dryer vents exhaust outside, not into the basement
- Do not store firewood in the basement during summer
- Check that any sump pump is operational before storm season

### Inspect Exterior Concrete

Summer is when patios, driveways, sidewalks, and stoops show new cracks from winter damage. These exterior surfaces matter because cracked concrete adjacent to your foundation can direct water toward rather than away from it.

Look for:
- Settled concrete sections that now slope toward the house
- Cracks in walkways or patios that connect to the foundation
- Gaps between steps or stoops and the foundation wall

## When to Schedule Fall Repairs

The best time to repair foundation cracks is before winter, not during it. If your summer monitoring reveals:

- New cracks that were not there in spring
- Existing cracks that have grown
- Persistent moisture problems despite good grading and drainage
- Any structural concerns like bowing or displacement

Schedule an inspection in September or October. This gives you time to complete repairs before the ground freezes and the spring thaw brings water pressure against your foundation.
